Section 1: Introduction to Mechanisms

Mechanisms are devices that convert one motion or force into another. They can be simple — like door handles, scissors and light switches — or complex, as used in industrial robots, cars and bikes.

Mechanisms play a vital role in industry. Most industrial processes have electronic control systems but rely on the movement of mechanisms to do work: pressing large steel sheets, lifting components and cutting material. Moving parts of a machine are potential trap hazards and so guards or covers are used to keep people safe.

All mechanisms: involve motion • involve force • should make a job easier • need an input to work • produce an output.

Types of motion

There are four basic kinds of motion:

🔄
Rotary
Turning in a circle.
e.g. wheels, clock hands, gears
➡️
Linear
Movement in a straight line.
e.g. paper trimmer, lift moving between floors
⇄
Reciprocating
Backwards and forwards in a straight line.
e.g. sewing machine needle, car engine piston
🏋️
Oscillating
Swinging backwards and forwards in an arc.
e.g. clock pendulum, playground swing

Section 2: Gears

Gears are toothed wheels designed to transmit rotary motion and power from one part of a mechanism to another. They are used to increase or decrease output speed, and can also change the direction of rotation. Gears are fitted to shafts using keys or splines so they rotate together. The most common type is the spur gear.

Simple gear trains

When two or more gears mesh together they form a gear train. The input gear is called the driver; the output gear is called the driven. The teeth on the driver effectively push the teeth on the driven gear.

Meshing gears rotate in opposite directions. If the driver is clockwise, the driven is anticlockwise.
📌
Diagram: Simple gear train — driver gear A (large) meshing with driven gear B (small), showing contact point C and direction arrows
Filename: gear-simple-train.png

If gear A (driver) has 24 teeth and makes one full revolution, its 24 teeth pass the contact point. This pushes gear B around — and because gear B only has 12 teeth, it makes two complete revolutions for every one of gear A.

The gear equation

TeethIn × TurnsIn = TeethOut × TurnsOut
Turns can be revolutions per second (rev s−1) or per minute (rev min−1)
Worked Example 1 — Find the output speed

A driver gear has 48 teeth and rotates at 10 rev s−1. The driven gear has 20 teeth. Find its speed.

1
Write the equation: TeethIn × TurnsIn = TeethOut × TurnsOut
2
Substitute: 48 × 10 = 20 × TOut
3
Solve: TOut = (48 × 10) ÷ 20 = 24 rev s−1
Worked Example 2 — Find the number of teeth

A motor gear has 36 teeth and rotates at 380 rev s−1 clockwise. The driven gear rotates at 152 rev s−1. How many teeth does the driven gear have?

1
36 × 380 = TOut × 152
2
TOut = (36 × 380) ÷ 152 = 90 teeth

Section 3: Velocity Ratio in Gears

The velocity ratio (VR) describes how much the speed changes between the driver and driven gear. It is calculated using the number of teeth:

VR = TeethOut ÷ TeethIn = SpeedIn ÷ SpeedOut
Remember: the teeth equation is the opposite way up to the speed equation.
Think: Driven (passenger's teeth) ÷ Driver (bus driver's teeth)
Worked Example 1 — Speed increase (VR < 1)
📌
Filename: gear-vr-increase.png

Gear A: 24 teeth (driver). Gear B: 12 teeth (driven).

1
VR = TeethOut ÷ TeethIn = 12 ÷ 24 = 0.5 (or 1:2)
2
If gear A rotates at 90 rev min−1 CW, gear B rotates at 180 rev min−1 ACW.
Worked Example 2 — Speed decrease (VR > 1)
📌
Diagram: Gear A (8 teeth, driver) meshing with Gear B (24 teeth, driven) — speed decrease
Filename: gear-vr-decrease.png

Gear A: 8 teeth (driver). Gear B: 24 teeth (driven).

1
VR = 24 ÷ 8 = 3 (or 3:1)
2
If gear A rotates at 90 rev min−1 CW, gear B rotates at 30 rev min−1 ACW.

Section 4: Idler Gears

To make the driven gear rotate in the same direction as the driver, a third gear — called an idler gear — is inserted between them.

The idler gear has no effect on the velocity ratio of the system. It only changes the direction of the output. The size of the idler does not matter.
📌
Diagram: Three-gear train — Gear A (driver, 24 teeth) → Gear B (idler, small) → Gear C (driven, 12 teeth), showing all three rotating CW
Filename: gear-idler.png

In the diagram above, gears A and C rotate in the same direction. The VR is still calculated from driver A to driven C only — the idler is ignored: VR = 12 ÷ 24 = 0.5.

Section 5: Compound Gear Trains

Simple gear trains are limited in the velocity ratio they can produce — very large or very small speed changes require impractically large gears. A compound gear train solves this by mounting pairs of gears on the same shaft (the intermediate shaft), multiplying the gearing effect in a much smaller space.

In a compound gear train, two gears on the same intermediate shaft rotate together at the same speed. The total VR is found by multiplying the individual VRs.
📌
Diagram: Plan view of compound gear train — Input gear (20 teeth) meshes with large gear (80 teeth) on intermediate shaft; small gear (10 teeth) on same shaft meshes with output gear (60 teeth)
Filename: gear-compound-plan.png

Calculating compound gear VR

Worked Example — Compound gear train

Input gear: 20 teeth. Large gear on intermediate shaft: 80 teeth. Small gear on intermediate shaft: 10 teeth. Output gear: 60 teeth. Input speed: 240 rev s−1.

1
VR of first pair: VR1 = 80 ÷ 20 = 4
2
VR of second pair: VR2 = 60 ÷ 10 = 6
3
Total VR: VRtotal = VR1 × VR2 = 4 × 6 = 24
4
Output speed: SpeedOut = SpeedIn ÷ VR = 240 ÷ 24 = 10 rev s−1

Section 6: Worm and Wheel

A worm gear is another way to achieve large speed reductions. The worm looks like a screw thread and is fixed to the driver shaft. It meshes with a worm wheel fixed to the driven shaft, which runs at 90° to the driver shaft.

For calculation purposes, treat the worm as a single-tooth gear. One full rotation of the worm advances the worm wheel by exactly one tooth.
📌
Diagram: Worm gear meshing with a 30-tooth worm wheel, showing 90° change in shaft direction
Filename: gear-worm-wheel.png
Worked Example — Worm and wheel

A worm meshes with a 30-tooth worm wheel. The motor rotates at 100 rev s−1. Find the output speed.

1
VR: TeethOut ÷ TeethIn = 30 ÷ 1 = 30:1
2
Output speed: 100 ÷ 30 = 3.33 rev s−1

Section 7: Belt Drives

Belt drives transmit rotary motion between shafts that may be far apart. A belt wraps around two or more pulleys — thin metal discs with a groove cut into the rim. The belt is tensioned by pulling one pulley out and locking it in place.

Unlike meshing gears, belt drives do not reverse the direction of rotation. To reverse direction, the belt must be crossed between the pulleys.
📌
Diagram: Two pulleys connected by an open belt (same direction) and a crossed belt (reversed direction), showing driver and driven pulleys with diameter labels
Filename: belt-open-crossed.png

Belt drive equation

DiameterDriver × TurnsDriver = DiameterDriven × TurnsDriven

Velocity ratio for belt drives

VR = DiameterDriven ÷ DiameterDriver
Output speed = Input speed ÷ VR
Worked Example — Belt drive VR and output speed

Driver pulley diameter: 120 mm. Driven pulley diameter: 40 mm. Input speed: 1200 rev/min.

1
VR: 40 ÷ 120 = 1/3 (or 1:3)
2
Output speed: 1200 ÷ (1/3) = 3600 rev/min (speed increase because driven pulley is smaller)

Types of belt

Flat / V-belt
  • Made from rubber and string composite
  • Inexpensive and easy to replace
  • No lubrication needed
  • Absorbs shock loads by slipping
  • Can slip if over-tensioned or worn
Toothed belt
  • Interlocking teeth prevent slipping
  • Used where slippage would cause damage
  • e.g. car timing belt — prevents pistons colliding with valves
  • Quiet, low maintenance
  • More expensive than flat belts

Jockey pulley

A jockey (tensioner) pulley is a spring-loaded pulley used to keep the belt correctly tensioned. Too slack and the belt slips; too tight and the bearings wear out.

📌
Diagram: Belt drive system with spring-loaded jockey pulley showing tensioning arrangement
Filename: belt-jockey-pulley.png

Stepped-cone pulleys

A variety of output speeds can be achieved without changing pulleys by using stepped-cone pulleys — a series of pulleys of different diameters fixed to the same shaft. Moving the belt to a different step changes the speed ratio.

📌
Diagram: Stepped-cone pulley system showing belt at different positions for different speed ratios
Filename: belt-stepped-cone.png

Section 8: Chain Drives

Where large forces must be transmitted with no slippage, a chain drive is used. Instead of a pulley, a toothed wheel called a sprocket drives a chain, which in turn drives another sprocket. Chain drives do not slip.

Advantages of chain drives
  • Very strong — will not slip under normal conditions
  • Transmits large forces reliably
  • Positive drive — no loss of motion
Disadvantages of chain drives
  • Must be oiled regularly
  • Chain and sprockets prone to wear
  • More expensive than belt drives
  • Noisier than belt drives
📌
Diagram: Bicycle chain drive — pedal sprocket (large, driver) connected by chain to rear wheel sprocket (small, driven), with direction arrows
Filename: chain-bicycle.png

Chain drive equation

TeethDriver × TurnsDriver = TeethDriven × TurnsDriven
VR = TeethDriven ÷ TeethDriver
Worked Example — Bicycle chain drive VR

A bicycle has 60 teeth on the pedal sprocket (driver) and 10 teeth on the rear wheel sprocket (driven). Find the VR.

1
VR = TeethDriven ÷ TeethDriver = 10 ÷ 60 = 1/6 (or 1:6)
2
For every 1 revolution of the pedals, the wheel turns 6 times — a speed increase.

Derailleur gears

Racing bikes and mountain bikes use Derailleur gears — a spring-loaded jockey wheel that keeps the chain tensioned while allowing it to be moved between different sprockets on a cassette (rear hub) and different chainrings (front). This gives a wide range of gear ratios for different terrains.

📌
Diagram: Derailleur gear system showing rear cassette sprockets, chain, and spring-loaded jockey wheel tensioner
Filename: chain-derailleur.png

Section 9: Converting Motion

Many mechanisms involve changing one type of motion into another. For example, the rotary motion of a pillar-drill handle is changed to the linear motion of the drill bit moving down.

Cams

A cam is a specially shaped piece of metal or plastic that converts input rotary motion into output oscillating or reciprocating motion. The cam works by guiding a follower held against it by its own weight or a spring.

There are two main types of cam:

  • Circular (eccentric) cam — produces a smooth, continuous rise and fall of the follower
  • Pear-shaped cam — has a dwell period (the follower is stationary for about half a revolution) followed by a rapid rise and fall. Used on motor-car camshafts to operate engine valves.
📌
Diagram: Side-by-side comparison of circular/eccentric cam and pear-shaped cam with follower, showing crown (highest point), heel (lowest point) and dwell period
Filename: cam-types-comparison.png

Cam terminology

  • Crown — the highest point of the cam profile (follower at maximum height, valve fully open)
  • Heel — the lowest point of the cam profile (follower at rest, valve closed)
  • Dwell period — the part of the cam rotation where the follower is stationary
  • Stroke — the distance between the crown and heel (= the distance the valve opens)

Section 10: Crank and Slider

A crank-and-slider mechanism converts between rotary and reciprocating motion. The crank rotates while the slider (piston) reciprocates in a straight line. The longer the crank, the further the slider moves.

📌
Diagram: Crank-and-slider showing (left) reciprocating-to-rotary motion as in a car engine piston/crankshaft; (right) rotary-to-reciprocating as in a power hacksaw with labelled crank, connecting rod and slider
Filename: crank-slider-both.png

The crank-and-slider is used in two main ways:

  • Reciprocating → Rotary: Car engines — pistons connected to a crankshaft by connecting rods. As pistons move down in sequence they keep the crankshaft turning.
  • Rotary → Reciprocating: Power hacksaw — a motor drives a crank connected to a saw frame that slides back and forth on an arm.

Section 11: Rack and Pinion

A rack and pinion mechanism converts rotary motion to linear motion (or vice versa). A round spur gear (the pinion) meshes with a flat toothed bar (the rack).

📌
Diagram: Rack and pinion — showing pinion gear rotating on fixed centre causing rack to move linearly, with arrows indicating direction of motion
Filename: rack-pinion.png

There are three ways a rack and pinion can be used:

  1. Rack moves linearly → pinion rotates (e.g. gripper mechanism)
  2. Pinion rotates on fixed centre → rack moves linearly (e.g. camera tripod, pillar drill)
  3. Rack is fixed, pinion rotates → pinion's centre moves linearly (e.g. lathe carriage)
