Section 1: Pneumatic Systems

Introduction

Pneumatics is used widely in industry and in everyday life. You would expect to see pneumatic systems in factories, production lines and processing plants, where they are used to move, hold or shape objects.

Every pneumatic system makes use of compressed air. Compressed air is the air that we breathe forced into a smaller space. We can use the energy stored in this compressed air to do useful work.

All pneumatic systems can be described using a simple systems diagram:

Input (compressed air) → Process (valves and cylinders) → Output (linear motion or force)

Advantages of pneumatics

Pneumatics is chosen over other systems such as hydraulics and electrics for a number of reasons:

✅ Clean
Uses compressed air — any leaks are harmless. Suitable for food production.
✅ Safe
No sparks — suitable for paint spraying and explosive environments.
✅ Reliable
Components last a long time with few breakdowns.
✅ Flexible
Components can be reconfigured for different tasks easily.

Section 2: Cylinders

Cylinders are the ‘muscles’ of pneumatic systems. They convert the energy stored in compressed air into linear motion — movement in a straight line. There are two types you need to know.

Single-acting cylinder (SAC)

A single-acting cylinder requires only one air supply. When compressed air is supplied, it pushes the piston and the cylinder outstrokes (goes positive). When the air is removed, a spring inside returns the piston — the cylinder instrokes (goes negative).

SAC symbol: a rectangle with a spring on one side and a single port.

Section 3: Valves

Valves control the flow of compressed air to cylinders. They can turn air on or off, change its direction, or slow it down.

The 3/2 valve

A 3/2 valve has 3 ports and 2 states. A port is a connection point for a pipe. A state is a position the valve can be in.

PortNameDescription
1Main airConnected to the compressed air supply (manifold)
2OutputConnected to the cylinder or next component
3ExhaustAllows trapped air to escape when the cylinder instrokes

States of a 3/2 valve

State 1 (Off/Unactuated): Main air is blocked. Any air in the cylinder can exhaust through port 3, allowing the cylinder to instroke.

State 2 (On/Actuated): Main air flows from port 1 to port 2, supplying the cylinder.

Actuators

An actuator is the mechanism used to change the state of a valve. Common types include:

  • Push button — pressed by an operator, spring return
  • Roller — tripped by a moving cylinder (used in automatic circuits)
  • Pilot air — changed by a pulse of compressed air from another valve
  • Solenoid — operated electrically

The 5/2 valve

A 5/2 valve has 5 ports and 2 states. It is most commonly used to control a double-acting cylinder as it provides a constant air supply to keep the piston in position.

PortDescription
1Main air supply
2Output — typically connected to instroke the DAC
3Exhaust from port 2 side
4Output — typically connected to outstroke the DAC
5Exhaust from port 4 side
The 5/2 valve holds the piston in position because it always supplies air to one side of the cylinder, even when not being actuated.

Section 4: Simple Circuits

Car park barrier — SAC circuit

A car park has a barrier controlled by a single-acting cylinder. The attendant presses a button to raise it.

  • When the button is pressed, the 3/2 valve actuates and supplies air to the SAC — the cylinder outstrokes and raises the barrier.
  • When released, the valve returns to its off state and the spring instrokes the piston, lowering the barrier.

Section 6: Restrictors

Cylinders can move very quickly, which may be dangerous or cause problems in a circuit. A unidirectional restrictor slows the airflow in one direction only.

SQA spec note: You only need to know the unidirectional restrictor for N5. The bi-directional (throttle) valve is not in the specification.

The unidirectional restrictor works by:

  • Air flowing into port 1 — a ball blocks the bypass route, air is forced through the restriction → slowed down
  • Air flowing into port 2 — the ball moves away, air flows freely → unrestricted
We always restrict the exhaust air leaving a cylinder. This gives a smoother, more controlled piston movement.

Section 8: OR Control

OR control uses two 3/2 valves connected in parallel with a shuttle valve. The cylinder outstrokes when valve A OR valve B is pressed. This allows a circuit to be operated from more than one location.

The shuttle valve

A shuttle valve contains a small ball that is blown from side to side. It allows air from either input to reach the output, preventing air from backflowing into the other valve.

⚠️ A T-piece cannot replace a shuttle valve in an OR circuit — air would backflow through the inactive valve and exhaust, preventing the cylinder from operating.

Section 9: Time Delay

Section 11: Forces in Pneumatic Cylinders

Single-acting cylinder force

The force produced by a cylinder depends on two things: the air pressure and the surface area of the piston.

Force (N) = Pressure (N/mm²) × Area (mm²)
Rearranging: Pressure = Force ÷ Area   |   Area = Force ÷ Pressure

The piston is circular, so its area is calculated using:

Area = πr² = π(d/2)²
where r = radius and d = diameter of the piston
Worked example — SAC outstroke force

Air is supplied to a single-acting cylinder at a pressure of 4 N/mm². The piston diameter is 25 mm. Calculate the outstroke force.

1
Write down the known values:
Pressure = 4 N/mm², Diameter = 25 mm
2
Calculate the piston area:
Area = π × (25/2)² = π × 12.5² = π × 156.25 = 491 mm²
3
Calculate the force:
Force = Pressure × Area = 4 × 491 = 1964 N = 1.96 kN

Double-acting cylinder forces

In a double-acting cylinder, the outstroke force is greater than the instroke force. This is because during the instroke, the piston rod occupies some of the piston area, reducing the effective area the air pushes against.

Effective area = Piston area − Piston rod area
Instroke force = Pressure × Effective area
Worked example — DAC outstroke and instroke forces

A DAC has a piston diameter of 25 mm and piston rod diameter of 5 mm. Pressure = 4 N/mm².

1
Known values: Pressure = 4 N/mm², Piston ⌀ = 25 mm, Rod ⌀ = 5 mm
2
Piston area: π × 12.5² = 491 mm²
3
Outstroke force: 4 × 491 = 1964 N
4
Rod area: π × 2.5² = 19.6 mm²
5
Effective area: 491 − 19.6 = 471.4 mm²
6
Instroke force: 4 × 471.4 = 1885.6 N
